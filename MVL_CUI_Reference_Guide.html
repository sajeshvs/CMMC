What Is CUI and Why Does It Matter?

CUI stands for Controlled Unclassified Information. It is government-created or government-provided information that, while not classified (Secret/Top Secret), still requires specific safeguarding and handling controls mandated by federal regulation.

📄 What CUI Looks Like at MVL

  • Requests for Proposal (RFPs) from U.S. Government agencies
  • Contract award documents and modifications
  • Pricing data, cost proposals, and financial details from government contracts
  • Technical drawings or specifications marked with CUI banners
  • Documents bearing markings such as "CUI", "FOUO", or distribution statements

⚖ Why We Must Protect It

  • Legal obligation: DFARS 252.204-7012 requires all DoD contractors to protect CUI
  • CMMC certification: MVL must achieve CMMC Level 2, which requires implementing 110 security controls
  • Contract eligibility: Failure to protect CUI can result in loss of current and future government contracts
  • National security: CUI, if aggregated or leaked, can compromise defense programs

👥 Who Handles CUI at MVL?

Not every MVL employee handles CUI. A defined group of authorized personnel — including those in proposals, project delivery, procurement, finance, and executive leadership — are identified and granted access through role-based security groups.

If you are part of the CUI-authorized team, you will have specific access rights and are expected to follow the procedures outlined in this guide.

Our Platform — Microsoft 365 GCC G5

All CUI at MVL is processed exclusively within Microsoft 365 GCC G5 — a government-grade cloud environment authorized by FedRAMP. This ensures U.S. data residency, logical separation from commercial Microsoft tenants, and background-screened Microsoft personnel.

☁ What GCC G5 Means for You

  • Your data stays in the U.S. — all CUI is stored on servers within the continental United States
  • Separate from commercial cloud — MVL's GCC tenant is logically isolated from standard Microsoft 365
  • Government-validated security — FedRAMP Moderate authorization means the platform meets rigorous federal standards
  • Built-in protection tools — sensitivity labels, DLP, encryption, and audit logging are all native to this environment

🔒 The Tools That Protect CUI

  • Microsoft Entra ID — manages who can log in and under what conditions (MFA, device checks)
  • Microsoft Purview — applies sensitivity labels to documents and enforces data loss prevention (DLP) policies
  • Microsoft Intune — ensures only company-managed, encrypted devices can access CUI
  • Microsoft Defender — provides continuous threat detection on endpoints
  • Microsoft Sentinel — monitors all CUI-related activity and raises alerts on suspicious behavior
  • Zscaler — secures network traffic and enforces web/app access policies

How CUI Flows Through MVL

CUI follows a controlled lifecycle — from government receipt, through internal processing and distribution, to secure submission back to the government. Every stage occurs within the GCC G5 boundary.

◉ Pre-Award Flow (Proposals & Bids)

  1. Government sends RFP/RFI via secure portals or email
  2. Authorized CUI Receiver downloads and applies a CUI sensitivity label
  3. Document is stored in the Pre-Award Library on SharePoint GCC
  4. Proposal team members access the document through role-based permissions — no file duplication needed
  5. Subcontractor/supplier sharing happens only through approved B2B guest access with DLP enforcement
  6. Final proposal is submitted back to the government via PIEE/WAWF or secure email

◉ Post-Award Flow (Contracts & Delivery)

  1. Government issues award documents via DoD SAFE or email
  2. Executives review, sign, and return the award to the government
  3. The CUI handler creates two versions:
    • Full version — labeled CUI, shared with the Finance team
    • Redacted version — labeled CUI-Redacted, shared with Project Managers and site teams
  4. Project Managers distribute redacted CUI downstream to coordination teams on a need-to-know basis
  5. All access is governed by Entra role-based security groups with DLP enforcement

How We Protect CUI — Four Security Layers

MVL applies a "Zero Trust" approach to CUI protection. This means no one is trusted by default — every access request is verified across four layers before CUI can be reached.

🔑 Layer 1 — Identity Verification

What it means for you: Every time you access CUI, the system verifies who you are, what device you're on, and where you're logging in from.

  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is required for every CUI user — no exceptions
  • Conditional Access checks your device compliance, location, and sign-in risk before granting access
  • Legacy authentication methods (basic auth, older app logins) are completely blocked
  • Admin access is time-limited and requires additional approval

💻 Layer 2 — Device Protection

What it means for you: Your device must be company-managed and encrypted before you can touch CUI.

  • Only Intune-managed devices can access CUI — personal devices are blocked
  • BitLocker encryption is mandatory on all endpoints
  • USB write access is restricted to prevent copying CUI to removable media
  • Defender for Endpoint continuously monitors for threats
  • Lost or stolen devices can be remotely wiped

🛡 Layer 3 — Data Protection (Labels & DLP)

What it means for you: Every CUI document is labeled, encrypted, and monitored. The system actively prevents CUI from leaving authorized channels.

  • Sensitivity labels classify and encrypt documents — the CUI label restricts who can open, edit, and forward content
  • DLP policies block CUI from being emailed externally, shared via unapproved links, copied to USB drives, or printed without authorization
  • Content markings (headers, footers, watermarks) make CUI visually identifiable
  • All CUI actions are logged for audit purposes

💬 Layer 4 — Collaboration Controls

What it means for you: CUI can only be discussed and shared in approved channels — never in public Teams channels or through anonymous sharing links.

  • CUI SharePoint libraries are logically isolated with role-based permissions
  • Teams channels used for CUI discussion are private only
  • Guest access for subcontractors goes through approved B2B channels with strict controls
  • Anonymous or "anyone with the link" sharing is completely disabled for CUI content

Sensitivity Labels — Data Classification

Every document and email at MVL must carry a sensitivity label. These labels determine the level of encryption, who can access the content, and what content markings are applied. Think of them as color-coded security levels for your files.

Label What It Means Encryption Content Marking Who Can Access
CUI Government-controlled information requiring highest protection AES-256 + Rights Management Header: "CUI" • Footer: "Controlled — Do Not Distribute" • Watermark Authorized CUI groups only
CUI-Redacted Sanitized version of CUI with sensitive details removed AES-256 Header: "CUI — REDACTED" • Footer with handling notice Broader internal teams (project delivery)
Confidential Business-sensitive information (not government CUI) AES-256 Header: "Confidential" • Footer All MVL employees
Internal General internal use — default label for new documents Optional Footer: "Internal Use Only" All MVL employees
Public Approved for public release — no restrictions None None Unrestricted

🏷 Label Priority — Highest Wins

Labels follow a strict priority order. When you attach a CUI-labeled file to an email, the email automatically inherits the CUI label. You cannot downgrade a label without providing a written justification, which is logged for audit.

/* Label priority (highest → lowest) */ 5. CUI → Highest protection 4. CUI-Redacted → Sanitized CUI 3. Confidential → Business-sensitive 2. Internal → Standard internal 1. Public → No restrictions

🔒 What the CUI Label Actually Does

When you apply the CUI label to a document, the following protections are automatically enforced:

  • Encryption: Only members of authorized CUI security groups can open the file
  • Permissions: View and Edit only — no forwarding, no printing (for email)
  • Offline access: Limited to 7 days — after that, the device must reconnect to verify access rights
  • Visible markings: Header, footer, and watermark are added automatically
  • Downgrade protection: If you try to remove or lower the label, you must provide a business reason
💡
Tip: If a document was received from a government source, contains government contract data, or has CUI markings — it should carry the CUI label. When in doubt, apply the higher label and consult IT.

Data Loss Prevention (DLP) — What Gets Blocked & Why

DLP policies are automated guardrails that prevent CUI from accidentally (or intentionally) leaving the authorized environment. These run silently in the background, and you'll only notice them when an action is blocked or you receive a policy tip.

Scenario What Happens Why
Emailing CUI externally to a non-approved domain 🛑 Blocked — you'll see a policy tip explaining the restriction CUI can only be sent to pre-approved external domains
Sharing CUI via SharePoint/OneDrive with someone outside MVL 🛑 Blocked — override available with business justification External sharing must go through approved guest access
Accessing CUI from an unmanaged/personal device 🛑 Download blocked — view-only access may be allowed CUI must remain on encrypted, company-managed endpoints
Sharing CUI in Teams with external guests not on the approved list ⚠ Blocked for external, warned for internal Teams CUI channels are restricted to authorized users
Copying CUI to a USB drive 🛑 Blocked — notification appears on your desktop Removable media is a high-risk exfiltration vector
Printing CUI documents ⚠ Restricted — logged if printing is enabled Physical copies of CUI must be controlled and tracked
Sending any file externally without a label 🛑 Blocked — you'll be prompted to add a label first All outbound content must be classified before it can leave MVL
⚠
Important: If you're blocked by a DLP policy and believe the action is legitimate, don't try to work around it. Contact IT Security to request an exception. Every DLP event is logged in our security monitoring system, and workarounds can trigger an incident investigation.

How to Apply Sensitivity Labels

Labeling is mandatory. No document or email can be saved or sent without a sensitivity label. Here's how to apply them in the tools you use every day.

📄 In Word, Excel & PowerPoint

1

Open the Sensitivity Menu

Look for the Sensitivity button in the ribbon bar (Home tab). Click it to see the list of available labels.

2

Select the Right Label

Choose the label that matches the content: CUI for government-controlled content, Confidential for business-sensitive, or Internal for general use.

3

Save — Label Travels With the File

Once applied, the label persists even when the file is shared, moved, or emailed. Headers, footers, and watermarks are applied automatically.

📧 In Outlook (Email)

1

Click Sensitivity in the Ribbon

When composing a new email or replying, click the Sensitivity button in the message ribbon.

2

Choose the Label

Select the label that matches your email's content. If you attach a CUI-labeled document, the email will automatically inherit the CUI label.

3

Watch for Policy Tips

If you try to send CUI to someone outside the approved domain list, you'll see a DLP policy tip explaining why it's blocked.

🌐 In SharePoint & Teams

  • Uploading to CUI libraries: Files uploaded to CUI SharePoint libraries automatically receive the library's default CUI label. Always verify the label in the file properties panel after upload.
  • Creating new documents: Documents created directly within a CUI library inherit the CUI label automatically.
  • Files shared in Teams: Files shared in CUI Teams channels are stored in the associated SharePoint library and follow the same labeling rules.

🤖 Automatic Labeling — The System Helps You

MVL has configured automatic detection that scans documents and emails for CUI indicators — such as CUI markings, DFARS clause numbers, distribution statements, and content from government (.mil / .gov) senders.

  • If CUI content is detected, you'll see a banner recommending you apply the CUI label
  • In some cases, the system will auto-apply the label without requiring your action
  • Background scanning also catches CUI in existing files that may have been missed

Auto-labeling is a safety net, not a replacement for your judgment. Always review and verify that the correct label is applied to your documents.

CUI Handling — Do's & Don'ts

A quick-reference guide for everyday CUI handling. If you're ever unsure, err on the side of caution and reach out to IT Security.

✅ Do

  • Always apply the correct sensitivity label before saving or sharing any document
  • Use SharePoint CUI libraries for storing and sharing CUI — this is the only authorized location
  • Verify recipient authorization before sending CUI content by email
  • Use private Teams channels when discussing CUI-related topics
  • Lock your screen when stepping away from your desk
  • Report any suspected CUI spillage immediately to IT Security
  • Complete your CUI training annually and stay current on policy updates
  • Ask IT Security if you're unsure whether something qualifies as CUI

❌ Don't

  • Never store CUI in Dropbox, Google Drive, personal OneDrive, or any commercial cloud — only GCC G5 SharePoint
  • Never email CUI to personal email accounts or non-approved external domains
  • Never copy CUI to USB drives, external hard drives, or personal devices
  • Never share CUI via anonymous or "anyone with the link" sharing
  • Never discuss CUI content in public Teams channels or general chat
  • Never print CUI without authorization — physical copies are tracked
  • Never forward CUI emails to people outside the authorized CUI groups
  • Never remove or downgrade a CUI label without a valid reason — every change is logged

If Something Goes Wrong — What To Do

Mistakes happen. What matters is how quickly you respond. If CUI ends up somewhere it shouldn't — an unauthorized email, a personal device, a commercial cloud service — that's called a "spillage," and it requires immediate action.

🚨 CUI Spillage — What You Should Do Immediately

1

Stop and Don't Make It Worse

Don't forward the email, don't share the file further, and don't try to "fix it" by deleting evidence. Contain the situation as-is.

2

Notify IT Security Immediately

Contact the IT Security team right away. Provide details on what happened: what CUI was involved, where it went, who may have received it, and when it occurred.

3

Let IT Handle the Investigation

IT Security will contain the incident, assess the scope, determine if government notification is required (DFARS mandates reporting within 72 hours for cyber incidents), and implement corrective actions.

4

Cooperate With the Review

You may be asked to provide additional details or participate in a lessons-learned review. This is not punitive — it helps strengthen our defenses for everyone.

⚠
Common Spillage Scenarios to Watch For: Accidentally emailing CUI to a personal address, saving CUI to a personal cloud storage account, sharing a CUI-labeled file to someone outside the authorized group, downloading CUI to an unmanaged device, or discussing CUI specifics in a public Teams channel with guests present.
💬
Remember: Reporting a potential incident early is always the right call. A quick report with a small scope is far better than a delayed discovery with a larger impact. MVL's incident response process is designed to resolve issues quickly, not to assign blame.
